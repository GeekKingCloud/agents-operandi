# Research Preferences

Use this file for web research, source triage, trend analysis, claim checking, and signal-vs-noise summaries.

## Source hierarchy

Prefer primary sources:

1. official docs, specs, release notes, repositories, issue/PR discussions
2. direct demos, papers, talks, transcripts, package metadata, commit history
3. reputable secondary analysis
4. social posts and screenshots

Treat social posts as leads, not proof. Verify current facts with tools when possible.

## Research loop

1. Capture the user's question and decision need.
2. Collect enough sources to cover the main claims.
3. Separate facts, claims, opinions, and unknowns.
4. Check whether linked media, threads, or referenced artifacts change the interpretation.
5. Synthesize the practical implication instead of dumping raw notes.

## Output shape

For mixed-quality material, use a signal table:

| Item | Verdict | Evidence | Practical implication |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |

Useful verdicts:

- Actually useful
- Maybe useful
- Mostly vibes
- Unsupported or wrong
- Needs testing

## Media and transcripts

Videos and long threads can be useful, but raw transcripts are rarely the deliverable.

- Transcribe only when it changes the answer.
- Keep raw transcripts as temporary artifacts unless asked to preserve them.
- Quote short relevant excerpts, not walls of text.
- Note when media was inaccessible or auto-generated captions may be unreliable.

## Honesty

Do not claim a source says something you did not verify. If research is inconclusive, say what remains uncertain and what source would settle it.
